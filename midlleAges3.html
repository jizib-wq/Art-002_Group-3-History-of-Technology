THE MIDDLE AGES & RENAISSANCE (500-1750)
Native Weapons and Metalworking (Defense Technology)


Inventor/Creator:
Early Filipino blacksmiths and warriors created traditional weapons such as the Kampilan using iron, bronze, and wood materials.


When was it made:
These weapons were widely developed and used during the pre-colonial and early colonial period (around 900–1700s).


Location:
They were used across different regions of the Philippines, especially in Mindanao, Luzon, and the Visayas.


Purpose:
These weapons were created for protection, warfare, and defense against rival groups and foreign invaders.


What was the use of it:
They were used in battles, tribal conflicts, and ceremonial displays of bravery and leadership among early Filipino communities.


How to use it:
To use weapons like the kampilan, warriors would grip the handle firmly and swing the blade in controlled motions during combat. Training and skill were important, as fighters needed to know proper stances, timing, and techniques to defend themselves and attack effectively.


How it works:
The kampilan works through the force and precision of its user. Its long, slightly curved blade is designed for powerful slashing strikes, while its weight distribution allows both speed and strength in combat. The sharp metal edge cuts through targets, and the sturdy handle ensures control during use.


Significance:
Native weapons and metalworking represent the strength, independence, and identity of early Filipino societies. These weapons are not only tools for combat but also symbols of honor, bravery, and craftsmanship. They reflect the advanced knowledge of metallurgy and the importance of protecting communities during times of conflict.
