THE INDUSTRIAL REVOLUTION (1750-1950)
Manila–Dagupan Railway (Transportation Technology)


Inventor/Creator:
The Manila–Dagupan Railway was constructed under the Spanish colonial government with engineering support from British contractors and Filipino laborers who helped build the railway infrastructure.


When was it made:
It was built in 1887 and officially opened in 1892, making it the first railway system in the Philippines during the late Industrial Revolution period.


Location:
The railway connected Manila in Luzon to Dagupan in Pangasinan, passing through several provinces in Central Luzon.


Purpose:
The railway was built to improve transportation of goods, agricultural products, and passengers across long distances more efficiently than horse-drawn carriages or boats.


What was the use of it:
It was used to transport rice, sugar, tobacco, and people between northern provinces and Manila, boosting trade and economic development during the colonial period.


How to use it:
To use the railway, passengers would purchase tickets and board trains at designated stations. Goods such as agricultural products were loaded onto freight cars. The train followed a fixed route, stopping at various stations to pick up and drop off passengers and cargo.


How it works:
The railway operated using steam-powered locomotives. Coal or wood was burned to heat water, producing steam that powered pistons and wheels, allowing the train to move along metal tracks. The tracks guided the train’s direction, ensuring efficient and stable travel across long distances.


Significance:
The Manila–Dagupan Railway marked the beginning of modern transportation in the Philippines. It greatly improved trade, mobility, and communication between regions. This innovation helped connect communities, supported economic growth, and introduced industrial technology that influenced future transportation systems in the country.
