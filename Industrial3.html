THE INDUSTRIAL REVOLUTION (1750-1950)
Tranvía Electric Tram System (Urban Transport Technology)


Inventor/Creator:
The electric tranvía (tram) system in Manila was introduced through the Manila Electric Railroad and Light Company (Meralco), spearheaded by American businessman Charles M. Swift, who won the franchise in 1903.


When was it made:
The tram system began operating in the late 19th century (around the 1880s–1890s) and expanded during the American colonial period up to the 1940s.


Location:
It operated in Manila and nearby urban areas such as Intramuros, Malate, and Binondo.


Purpose:
The tranvía was designed to provide fast and organized public transportation within the growing city of Manila.


What was the use of it:
It was used to carry passengers across urban districts, helping people travel to work, school, and commercial centers efficiently.


How to use it:
Passengers would wait at designated tram stops along city streets and board the electric streetcar when it arrived. They would pay a fare and take available seats while traveling along fixed routes.


How it works:
The tranvía worked using electric-powered streetcars that ran on metal rails installed on city roads. Electricity from overhead wires powered the motor, allowing smooth movement along fixed tracks while stopping to pick up and drop off passengers.


Significance:
The tranvía was one of the earliest forms of mass urban transportation in the Philippines. It modernized travel in Manila, reduced travel time, and supported the city’s economic and social growth. It also marked the beginning of electric-powered public transport in the country.
