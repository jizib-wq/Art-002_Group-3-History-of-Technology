Angono Petroglyphs (Prehistoric Rock Art)


Inventor/Creator:
The Angono Petroglyphs were carved by prehistoric Filipinos whose identities remain unknown but are believed to be early inhabitants of Luzon.


When was it made:
These rock carvings are estimated to have been created around 3,000 BCE or earlier, making them the oldest known artwork in the Philippines.


Location:
They are located in Binangonan, Rizal, near the town of Angono in Luzon.


Purpose:
The petroglyphs were likely created for symbolic, spiritual, or ritual purposes, possibly related to healing or early communication systems.


What was the use of it:
They were used as a form of expression, storytelling, or documentation of early beliefs and human experiences.


How to use it:
The petroglyphs were not “used” in a practical way like tools, but rather experienced and interpreted by people. Community members may have visited the site for rituals, storytelling, or spiritual practices.


How it works:
The petroglyphs were created by carving or engraving into soft rock surfaces using stone tools. By repeatedly scraping or chipping the rock, early artists formed simple human and symbolic figures.


Significance:
The Angono Petroglyphs are an important symbol of early Filipino creativity, culture, and belief systems.
